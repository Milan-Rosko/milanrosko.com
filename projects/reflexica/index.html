Summary

A locally witnessed procedure is quietly promoted into a claim about a completed domain. This operation may recur across mathematics, semantics, computation, and empirical inquiry. The promotion introduces no immediate contradiction. That is the problem. Reflexica is a program for diagnosing this specific structural passage.

What is Reflexica?

1 Introduction

Reflexica begins from an intuitionistic standpoint [Brouwer, 1913].

Read procedurally, Book IX, Proposition 20 of Euclid's Elements [Euclid, 1956] does not begin from a completed set of primes.

“Therefore $G$ is not the same with any one of the numbers $A, B,$ and $C$. And by hypothesis it is prime. Therefore the prime numbers $A, B, C,$ and $G$ have been found which are more than the assigned multitude of $A, B,$ and $C$.”

It supplies an extension rule: given any finite collection of primes, one can construct a number that must be divisible by a prime outside that collection. The proposition is an iterable and open-ended procedure before the contemporary presentation converts it into a claim about a completed domain. The difference is not one of attitude, but in metamathematical commitment.

The procedural reading is answerable to explicit construction: it commits only to what that construction reaches, and no further. The existential reading permits the result to be expressed over a completed domain, the primes as a total collection, rather than as an indefinitely iterable extension operation. One reading foregrounds the construction by which a further prime divisor is obtained; the other licenses quantification over the resulting domain. The difference is not that one is valid and the other invalid, but that they carry different proof-theoretic and semantic commitments.

Classically, this often appears cost-free: the existential formulation preserves the theorem while suppressing the difference between a proposition proved in a completed domain and a witness extracted from a construction. In constructive terms, the difference is between asserting $\exists x,P(x)$ and supplying an object $a:A$ together with evidence for $P(a)$; in type-theoretic notation, this is closer to inhabiting $\sum_{x:A}P(x)$ than to merely accepting an existential sentence. The relevant distinction here is between proof-theoretic signatures [Gentzen, 1935]. To establish that a number is composite, one produces a factor greater than one: a finite, extractable, checkable object. The witness supplies the computational content of the proof; in the case of compositeness, a factor pair $(a,b)$ is the finite object whose verification establishes the claim [Kleene, 1945]. Numbers alone, in a constructive sense, do not carry such a witness; it must be decided for them.

The asymmetry is visible in geometrically by the simple case bellow:

$$ \begin{array}{ll} 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+0+0\\[2ex] \exists \, \mathrm{d}_{i} >1 & \qquad ? \end{array} $$

We write schematically. Compositeness has the form

$$ \operatorname{Comp}(n) \;\iff\; \exists a,b\,(1<a<n \wedge 1<b<n \wedge n=ab), $$

so a proof may present a finite factor witness $(a,b)$. Primality is naturally expressed by bounded exclusion:

$$ \operatorname{Prime}(n) \;\iff\; n>1 \wedge \forall d\,(1<d<n \rightarrow d\nmid n). $$

The left arrangement witnesses factorization directly as the factor is read off the shape, while the right cannot provide a geometric primitive. No configuration of seven units exposes a nontrivial divisor, because there is none to exhibit. Primality must be established by exhaustive elimination, carried as a conclusion. The absence is not a gap in knowledge but a lack of structure—it is the feature that makes the witness asymmetry precise.

The lack of nontrivial factors is pulled through a theorem whose algebraic structure has already internalized the exclusion; the result is returned as a positive existential. The individual case remains decidable and well founded. What has occurred, however, is a small act of reification: an absence has been converted into a named object that can now be quantified over, composed into theorems, and treated as a feature of the domain rather than a local conclusion about a particular number. The conversion is conservative and introduces no contradiction. Here “conservative” is used diagnostically, not as a proved conservative-extension theorem: the promotion preserves local practice and introduces no immediate contradiction, while altering the semantic status of the object introduced. It is precisely this conservativeness that makes it easy to absorb—and easy to repeat at much larger scale without noticing that the character of the move has changed.

By the early 20th century, tensions of this kind could no longer be dismissed as a quirk of exposition [Gödel, 1931] [Heyting, 1931]. A theory may be locally impeccable and still carry a commitment in the way its existential conclusions are understood: a commitment that exceeds what the underlying procedure licenses. The difficulty is that the classical statement is also an epistemic posture, as the initial benign promissory reification at the level of a number, has been extended to the entire collection.

This identifies a pattern that recurs wherever local witness is taken to license global closure. A finite, positive, checkable construction may establish a local result cleanly, while the passage to a universal or completed claim is sustained by a structural assumption that the construction itself does not supply. Wherever a finite procedure is promoted into a statement about a total domain, the passage carries implicit weight.

2 Characterization

Reflexica is an interdisciplinary idea at the intersection of formal logic, natural sciences, and metamathematics. It investigates when empirical systems, models, and explanations treat the outputs or limits of local procedures as evidence for a completed domain not independently warranted by those procedures. Its central claim is that a systematic asymmetry may arise between locally witnessed procedures and the global closure conditions those procedures are taken to establish. In the cases considered below, this boundary often becomes visible only indirectly: through reification, self-relation, or obstruction at the point where local procedure is asked to support closure. The asymmetry does not impair mathematics or the sciences as they are ordinarily practiced; it becomes consequential at the periphery, where hard problems, foundational questions, and metamathematical phenomena converge, and the unexamined passage from local witness to global closure begins to carry implicit structural weight.

Reflexica is not a foundation, a replacement calculus, or a proposal to supersede classical mathematics aiming to replace Martin-Löf Type Theory [Martin-Löf, 1984], Bishop-Style Constructive Mathematics [Bishop, 1967], or constructive presentations of Homotopy Type Theory [Univalent Foundations Program, 2013], instead, we adopt a diagnostic posture that can be applied across settings—including classical and empiric ones—to mark the transition from local witness to global closure wherever that transition bears weight.

2.1 The Zeroth World

Impagliazzo's Possible-Worlds Taxonomy [Impagliazzo, 1995] names possible computational worlds by the structural relations among average-case hardness, one-way functions, public-key cryptography, and related complexity assumptions. The Zeroth World is not proposed as an additional world in that taxonomy. It names the missing or uninhabited position from which Reflexica asks why an unresolved method, once stabilized as a problem, can begin to function as semantic architecture.

The obstruction is not a technical limitation of specific formalisms or of the theoreticians working within them. It is a structural absence: a gap that certain classical principles reify locally, converting it into a reflection principle against which the system recurses on itself, rather than making contact with an independent domain that could ground the closure.

When does a system treat the success of its local operations as empirical warrant for a closure claim about the system, its domain, or an independently existing structure?

The difficulty lies in the conservativeness of the promotion. At a specific threshold, a theory begins to produce what functions as an essence claim after its intended closure has already been reached, or to seek a proposition that restores a transitivity the system has quietly lost. Neither move announces itself as a departure. Each reads as ordinary mathematical practice. It is this readability that allows the passage to go unmarked.

2.2 Methodology

We propose the following core methodological principles:

  • Limitative Abduction: infer not a hidden object, but a boundary condition, from repeated failure of a procedure to furnish the object its own semantics appear to require.
  • Mechanization, subject to exposition: where possible, express the suspected passage as a formal or computational dependency; where this is not yet possible, mark the claim as programmatic rather than established.
  • Parsimonious Semantics: do not introduce semantic objects beyond what the local procedure, witness, or model licenses, unless the promotion is made explicit.

These extend to the following characteristic preliminary instruments:

  • Problem Critique examines the substrate choices and commitments through which a problem or a field is constituted.
  • Semantic Liminality denotes a site where problems from distinct formal domains share propositional logic.
  • Sublimations are irreversible transitions from a formal to an empirical regime.

3 Topics

The cases below are not presented as identical instances. They are ordered to make increasingly formal versions of a proposed passage visible: local practice encounters an absence, obstruction, or bounded procedure; that local condition is stabilized or reified; and a demand for semantic closure is then placed upon it.

3.1 Mackie’s Error Theory as Sensitizing Pattern

Before turning to more formal cases, Mackie’s Error Theory [Mackie, 1977] can be used as a sensitizing pattern. It is not offered as a mathematical example of Reflexica. Rather, it helps isolate the kind of movement Reflexica is concerned with: a practice encounters an absence, stabilizes that absence through its own internal demands, and at some threshold permits the absence to appear as a semantic object. Mackie’s Queerness Argument is therefore treated not as proof of the Reflexica structure, but as an orientation toward it.

In the Mackiean sense, “queerness” concerns the metaphysical and epistemological strangeness of objective values. It serves here as a way of training attention toward a transition in which the absence of a required semantic term is disciplined by a practice and may then be treated as though it already named an object.

If the semantic object required by realist moral discourse could be produced in the relevant sense, Mackie’s Error Theory would fail; if the theory is correct, no such object is available to be produced. Its correctness therefore cannot take the form of a positive witness for the very object whose absence it asserts. What it marks is not a missing object subsequently available to inspection, but an absence whose being witnessed would destroy the thesis that identified it.

We therefore treat Mackie’s Queerness Argument as an indirect and defeasible indication of absence, not as an instance of Reflexica in the strict formal sense. Where a discourse requires a queer semantic object, the failure to produce or locate that object is not simply a gap awaiting repair.

The case attunes the reader to a possible movement from an internally stabilized absence to a purported semantic object, without establishing that the later formal cases share its structure. Whether the same passage recurs under more exact conditions remains the question that the following sections investigate.

3.2 Newcomb's Problem

Newcomb's Problem [Nozick, 1969] gives a more structured case. Here the issue is no longer moral objectivity, but the promotion of a locally stipulated predictor into a total evaluator over a class of evaluator-sensitive agents. Commonly treated within decision theory as a question of causal rationality, it is examined here through Problem Critique.

The agent may either “one-box”, receiving $B$, or “two-box”, receiving $A+B$, where $A=1000\,\mathrm{USD}$ and $B\in\{0,10^6\}\,\mathrm{USD}$ has already been fixed by a predictor $P$. Specifically, $B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}$ iff $P$ predicted one-boxing, and $B=0$ iff $P$ predicted two-boxing. Conditional on the value of $B$ being fixed, two-boxing strictly dominates one-boxing by $1000\,\mathrm{USD}$. However, let $C_1$ be the act of one-boxing and $C_2$ the act of two-boxing. If $p=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_1)$ and $q=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_2)$, then $\operatorname{EU}(C_1)=10^6p$ and $\operatorname{EU}(C_2)=1000+10^6q$. Hence one-boxing maximizes evidential expected utility iff $p-q>10^{-3}$.

Causal Decision Theory (CDT) [Lewis, 1981] treats $B$ as fixed and recommends $A+B$. Evidential Decision Theory (EDT) [Jeffrey, 1965] treats the act as evidence about the predictor's prior classification and recommends $B$, provided the evidential gap is sufficiently large. Both calculations are locally coherent. Neither addresses the prior question: whether the object being optimized over is well-formed.

We focus on the fact that the predictor is internalized as an evaluator over the same domain that contains the agent's decision procedure. The problem is then no longer merely that $P$ predicted $C$. It is that $C$ is allowed to be formed under the condition that $P$ evaluates $C$, while $P$ is simultaneously treated as ranging over such evaluator-sensitive choices. The object of evaluation is therefore not independent of the evaluating setup.

$$ P:\mathcal{C}(P)\to\{C_1,C_2\}, \quad C\in\mathcal{C}(P). $$

The dependency has an impredicative form: the choice is drawn from a space whose admissible description is already parameterized by the predictor that evaluates it. The agent's choice is not presented as a first-order act inside a fixed environment, but as an object partially constituted by its relation to the predictor. Once this is admitted, the predictor no longer functions as an ordinary antecedent.

The familiar disagreement between Causal Decision Theory and Evidential Decision Theory is therefore downstream. CDT restores predicativity by imposing a causal type distinction: the box is fixed, the act comes later, and no present intervention reaches backward. EDT instead collapses the predictor and the agent into one correlated evidential structure. Each response selects a different cut through the same impredicative substrate. Neither explains why such a substrate should have been admitted.

Reflexica's Problem Critique rejects this promotion. The first-order mechanism is admissible. What is not licensed is the promotion of a locally stipulated evaluator into a total evaluator over an open-ended class of agents whose decisions are themselves conditioned by that evaluation. A reliable predictor may still be stipulated externally for a particular case. The unlicensed totalization is precisely the passage from local procedure to global closure at issue here.

What remains is a bounded decision problem. One may specify a particular agent, a predictor with stated reliability over a fixed hypothesis class, and an explicit payoff structure; within that model, one may ask which action maximizes a selected decision rule, how calibration changes evidential and causal dependencies, and how robust the recommendation is under changes to the model. The restriction is not a ban on prediction, choice, or decision theory. It denies only the unlicensed promotion of a locally specified predictive mechanism into a total evaluator of an open-ended class of evaluator-sensitive agents.

3.3 Diagonalization

This chapter treats the classic provability predicate as a formal instance of how a locally witnessed procedure is promoted into a global semantic surrogate, and the resulting obstruction becomes visible only when the surrogate is forced into self-relation.

The standard reading of incompleteness often treats diagonalization and self-reference as the source of the pathology. Here the direction is reversed. Diagonalization does not introduce the defect; it exposes a promotion that was already present. The arithmetized provability predicate contains a numerical proxy for proofhood (HBL), sustained by an external interpretation of codes as proofs. It is the exposure, by self-reference, of a prior semantic promotion: the replacement of proofhood by coded provability, and then of coded provability by a modal or semantic surrogate for proof itself.

Incompleteness might not show that self-reference corrupts an otherwise stable semantic order; it shows that the semantic order was never internally contained by the coded predicate that claimed to represent it.

The legitimate core is local and procedural. Given a finite derivation $\pi$, one can check whether $\pi$ is a valid $T$-proof of a formula $\varphi$. Given a standard Gödel number $n$, one can decode it and verify whether it represents such a derivation.

Thus the proof relation $\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner)$ is not itself suspicious. It is a decidable arithmetical relation over finite codes. The problem begins when this local, checkable relation is promoted into a semantic account of proofhood.

Arithmetized provability is usually defined as:

$$ \operatorname{Prov}_T(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner) \;\equiv\; \exists n\,\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner). $$

This formula says that there exists a number satisfying the proof predicate. It does not directly say that a proof-object has been given. The semantic shift occurs when this existential over codes is read as the assertion that $\varphi$ is provable.

The apparent existential over proofs hides another layer. For a number $n$ to be a proof-code, it must decode into a sequence whose entries decode into formulas, whose steps decode into valid applications of inference rules.

$$ \exists n\,\exists\pi\, \bigl(\operatorname{Decode}(n)=\pi \wedge \operatorname{ValidProofSeq}_T(\pi,\varphi)\bigr). $$

But within the arithmetized setting, $\pi$ itself is typically represented numerically. The bottom layer is therefore not an actual proof-object, but another coded object whose interpretation depends on a metatheoretic convention.

The HBL predicate does not contain proofhood. It contains a numerical proxy for proofhood, plus an external convention under which certain numbers are read as proofs.

The Hilbert–Bernays–Löb Derivability Conditions are formally justified [Boolos, 1993]. They express the fact that coded provability is closed under certain proof-code transformations. But this is not the same as giving a semantics of proof-inhabitation.

For example, the condition

$$ \operatorname{Prov}_T(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner) \;\implies\: \operatorname{Prov}_T \bigl(\ulcorner\operatorname{Prov}_T(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner)\urcorner\bigr) $$

need not mean that a proof reflects on itself. It means that from a code satisfying the proof predicate one can construct another code satisfying the proof predicate for an existential claim about the first code.

HBL therefore gives closure of coded provability under coded proof-transformations. It does not, by itself, give an inhabited semantics of proofhood.

Nonstandard Witnesses and Unwitnessed Inhabitation

In a nonstandard model $M\models T$, the model may satisfy:

$$ M\models \exists n\,\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner). $$

But the witness may be nonstandard. Externally, it need not correspond to a genuine finite proof. Internally, the existential is inhabited; externally, the alleged proof may be non-extractable.

This reveals the central gap: the formal system may contain an object it treats as a proof-code, while the metatheory need not recognize that object as a genuine proof.

Diagonalization as Diagnostic

The Gödel Sentence has the form:

$$ G\iff \neg\operatorname{Prov}_T(\ulcorner \mathsf{G} \urcorner). $$

On the usual reading, the pathology lies in self-reference. On the Reflexica reading, diagonalization is not the pathology. It is the diagnostic device that reveals the prior promotion of coded provability into semantic proofhood.

The Gödel Sentence forces the provability predicate to occupy the position of proofhood itself. The resulting obstruction is not accidental. It marks the failure of the surrogate to contain the semantic core it was made to represent.

The Double Strength of the Diagonal

Diagonalization is powerful because it does not depend on the complete legitimacy of the semantic predicate it targets. Its diagnostic force persists under two opposed readings.

If the provability predicate is treated as merely formal or promissory, the diagonal exposes the gap between coded provability and genuine proofhood. The sentence

$$ G\iff \neg\operatorname{Prov}_T(\ulcorner \mathsf{G} \urcorner) $$

is then vindicated diagnostically: the surrogate does not capture the sentence through the semantic depth it was taken to represent. In schematic form, as a deflationary reading:

$$ \mathsf{G} \; \text{exposes the surrogate's vacuity}. $$

If, on the other hand, the classical semantic reading is granted—standard arithmetic, standard proof codes, and ordinary model-theoretic truth—then the same diagonal sentence is true in the usual Gödelian sense. Assuming $T$ is sufficiently strong, effectively axiomatized, and consistent, $G$ is not provable in $T$; because this is what $G$ asserts under the standard classical interpretation, $G$ is true:

$$ \mathsf{G} \; \text{expresses an unprovable arithmetical truth}. $$

The diagonal is robust across them because it records the boundary of the surrogate whether that surrogate fails to reach proofhood or succeeds well enough to receive its classical interpretation. It is again, self-similar.

The diagonal is true whether the semantic predicate is too weak to mean what it claims, or strong enough to be turned against itself.

Diagonalization is therefore a strong source of truth because its disclosure is invariant across the failure and qualified success of such a foundation. If the formal surrogate is empty, the diagonal reveals its emptiness. If the surrogate is semantically inhabited, the diagonal reveals its incompleteness.

Constructive Logic is attractive here because it refuses to let existence float free of witnesshood. An existential claim $\exists x:A\,P(x)$ must be inhabited by data, typically a pair consisting of a witness and evidence that the witness satisfies the predicate.

$$ (a,p):\sum_{x:A}P(x) $$

This does not eliminate limitative phenomena: Constructive Logic requires the semantic cost of existence claims to be paid in witnesses (or assumptions) rather than deferred into an ambient ontology of completed domains.

3.4 Self-Reference as a Schema

After diagonalization has been treated as the formal diagnostic case, failure by self-reference can be considered more broadly as a schema. that is, almost. Certain forms of self-reference reproduce the same configuration: a local procedure is asked to survey, name, or close over the totality to which it itself belongs.

This transfer from the strict diagonal case to less formal settings is programmatic. A structural resonance across algebra, computation, linguistic competence, and proof theory is not yet a proof that these domains instantiate one phenomenon. To assert such an identity without a witness would perform the totalizing move Reflexica is intended to diagnose.

Reflexica therefore does not claim that self-reference always causes obstruction or that every self-survey is a diagonal argument. It asks whether self-description, self-embedding, or self-similarity may reproduce a related demand for closure in domains that share no objects, methods, or historical contact at the relevant points of development [Lawvere, 1969].

Consider, as a schematic case, a Turing machine [Turing, 1937]

$$ \mathcal{M}=(Q,\Sigma,\Gamma,\delta,q_0,q_{\mathrm{acc}},q_{\mathrm{rej}}), $$

with language

$$ L(\mathcal{M})=\{w\in\Sigma^*: \mathcal{M}\text{ accepts }w\}. $$

The language is not an internal component of the transition table. It is a metatheoretic semantic extension assigned to the machine.

A particular computation may witness that a string belongs to $L(\mathcal{M})$. The language itself, however, is not another term available to the computation. It is the extension obtained when the machine's activity is taken as a whole [Tarski, 1956]. A self-description remains a presentation of the rule; a stream of accepted strings remains a sequence of witnesses. Neither returns the extension as an object internal to the activity that constitutes it.

A related question posed to a human provides a second schematic transfer.

How many words do you know?

The question is well formed, but an exact answer need not be available to the subject. Producing one would require an exhaustive, fixed inventory of an evolving and context-sensitive linguistic competence; the survey would be conducted by the very system whose contents were being inventoried. What returns is often an estimate rather than a retrieved census.

A language model asked the same question may also return an estimate. Such an estimate should be understood as a locally generated response rather than a retrieved census. Neither the human nor the language-model case establishes an identity with Gödelian diagonalization; each serves as a test of whether self-reference outside the strict diagonal case can reproduce a structurally related limit of self-survey.

The possible resonance with diagonalization appears only when that activity is required to yield its own closure. This is not a proof that the machine-human-LMM relation is of the same origin, it marks a question about recurrence: whether the demand presupposes a separation between the local procedure and the semantic extension constituted by taking that procedure as a whole.

3.5 Almost Saturated Fields

We consider the passage from proposition to runtime. Each transition selects one inhabitation of the space above it and discards what the layer below need not distinguish to proceed. The compression is constitutive, not incidental.

$$ P \;\xrightarrow{\;\lambda\;}\; \lambda x.\,t \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{compile}\;}\; s \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{eval}\;}\; r $$

Each arrow is many-to-one relative to the distinctions preserved at the next layer: different proofs may determine extensionally equivalent terms, different terms may compile to behaviorally equivalent states, and different states may evaluate to the same result. A lossless reversal would require preserving distinctions that the lower layer has already discarded. The obstacle is therefore not merely computational cost, but quotienting: the system proceeds by identifying cases that the previous layer could still distinguish.

Blum-Style Speedup results show that, for suitable complexity measures, there exist computable predicates for which no single program is optimal: any proposed implementation can be improved upon in the relevant formal sense [Blum, 1967]. A finite system reflecting on its own procedures can treat this as a model for improvement: self-reflection produces a stronger description, and the stronger description may yield a faster inhabitation of the same type. But the rate at which reflection produces distinguishable gain is itself subject to the same compression. Each reflective step purchases less potential than the last.

Let $(G_n)$ denote the improvement distinguishable by the system after $n$ rounds of reflection, relative to its current equivalence relation on procedures. The claim needed here is only diminishing distinguishable gain,

$$ \Delta_n=G_{n+1}-G_n,\quad \Delta_n\ge 0,\quad \Delta_n\to 0. $$

A logarithmic envelope,

$$ G(n)=\alpha\log(n+1)+\beta, $$

may be introduced only as a proposed model of saturation, not as a theorem following from speedup alone.

The fixed point of this process is “almost” saturation. A system reaches saturation not when it has exhausted its resources but when iterated self-reflection no longer produces an improvement distinguishable at its own level of abstraction. The evaluative closure the system operates within has become the ceiling. This is the due to the condition of being finite and self-reflective.

To locate the precise moment of promotion would itself require a witness outside the closure. The question of where the metric became the ceiling is not answerable from within the system asking it: a finite reflective system, seeking the fixed point of its own saturation, finds that the search is bounded by the very closure it seeks to locate.

4 The Program

The cases above share no substrate, method, or historical contact at the relevant points. What they share, if the proposal is correct, is a configuration: local production, self-relation or self-survey, demand for closure, and an ensuing reification or obstruction. Reflexica therefore proposes, rather than establishes, a structural position: recurrent self-relation, whether through self-survey or self-similar expansion, may coincide with a formation limit of related form. If such an obstruction is substrate-independent, then tools first developed in formal settings may have disciplined analogues outside those settings. The transfer, however, is itself a Reflexica-sensitive move: it must be marked, not assumed.

These rules might make the domain explicit before quantification ranges over it [Feferman, 1964], but typing disciplines can mark the transition from local witness to global closure rather than absorbing it silently. What type theory does internally for mathematics, Reflexica proposes as a posture toward the empirical: not to formalize the empirical cases, but to ask whether the structural obstruction identified inside formal systems has an analogue, or even an origin, in systems that model themselves.

Classical totalities and completed domains are undeniably productive, and their local utility is not in question. Our demand is narrower: that the passage from locally witnessed procedure to globally closed domain be marked at the point of transit, rather than absorbed silently into the semantics in which the next layer of reasoning is expressed.

The expansion to the empirical realm is the program's purpose. Neuronal computation, evolutionary closure, and self-modeling in artificial systems are not offered as proofs that a formal obstruction governs the empirical domain. They are offered as possible sites at which a related structural question may recur: whether phenomena that arise in distinct formal, computational, and empirical regimes are instances of one obstruction, rather than merely comparable cases described in a common vocabulary.

The epistemically proper form is consequently not:

These are all reflexica.

but:

Under what conditions would these count as the same phenomenon?

To assert their identity in advance would transform a series of bounded observations into an unwitnessed totality claim—the very passage Reflexica seeks to mark. The resonance therefore identifies a frontier at which formal logic, metamathematics, and the sciences of complex self-modeling systems converge on a question that none of them, separately, has the resources to settle under consistency.

Our question then becomes sharper:

Is reflexica inherited?

It asks whether a structural configuration—local production, self-relation or self-survey, a demand for closure, and an ensuing unwitnessed reification or obstruction—is preserved across a change of substrate. If it is, then Reflexica may name a substrate-independent limitative structure. If it is not, then the apparent resonance belongs to our description of the cases rather than to the cases themselves. Whether these are again an instance of Reflexica is the main object of study.

These questions are left open as the correct epistemic form for claims at the boundary of closure.
