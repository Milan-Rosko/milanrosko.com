Abstract

A locally witnessed procedure is quietly promoted into a claim about a completed domain. This operation may recur across mathematics, semantics, computation, and empirical inquiry. The promotion introduces no immediate contradiction. Reflexica is a program for diagnosing this specific structural passage.

What is Reflexica?

1 Introduction

Reflexica begins from an intuitionistic standpoint [Brouwer, 1913].

Read procedurally, Book IX, Proposition 20 of Euclid's Elements [Euclid, 1956] does not begin from a completed set of primes.

“Therefore $G$ is not the same with any one of the numbers $A, B,$ and $C$. And by hypothesis it is prime. Therefore the prime numbers $A, B, C,$ and $G$ have been found which are more than the assigned multitude of $A, B,$ and $C$.”

It supplies an extension rule: from any assigned finite multitude of primes, one constructs a number coprime to every member of that multitude. The proposition is an iterable and open-ended procedure before the contemporary presentation converts it into a claim about a completed domain. The difference concerns metamathematical commitment rather than attitude.

The procedural reading is answerable to explicit construction: it commits only to what that construction reaches, and no further. The existential reading permits the result to be expressed over a completed domain, the primes as a total collection, rather than as an indefinitely iterable extension operation. One reading foregrounds the construction by which a further prime divisor is obtained; the other licenses quantification over the resulting domain. Both readings are valid, though they carry different proof-theoretic and semantic commitments.

Classically, this often appears cost-free: the existential formulation preserves the theorem while suppressing the difference between a proposition proved in a completed domain and a witness extracted from a construction. The contrast is between a classically accepted existential $\exists x:A\,P(x)$ and its constructive interpretation as data: an object $a:A$ together with evidence for $P(a)$. Under propositions-as-types, this is represented by an inhabitant of $\sum_{x:A}P(x)$ [Howard, 1980]. The relevant distinction here is between proof-theoretic signatures [Gentzen, 1935]. To establish that a number is composite, one produces a factor greater than one: a finite, extractable, checkable object. The witness supplies the computational content of the proof; in the case of compositeness, a factor pair $(a,b)$ is the finite object whose verification establishes the claim [Kleene, 1945]. Numbers alone, in a constructive sense, do not carry such a witness; it must be decided for them.

The asymmetry is visible geometrically in the simple case below:

$$ \begin{array}{ll} 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+0+0\\[2ex] \exists \, \mathrm{d}_{i} >1 & \qquad ? \end{array} $$

We write schematically. Compositeness has the form

$$ \operatorname{Comp}(n) \;\iff\; \exists a,b\,(1<a<n \wedge 1<b<n \wedge n=ab), $$

so a proof may present a finite factor witness $(a,b)$. Primality is naturally expressed by bounded exclusion:

$$ \operatorname{Prime}(n) \;\iff\; n>1 \wedge \forall d\,(1<d<n \rightarrow d\nmid n). $$

The left arrangement witnesses factorization directly as the factor is read off the shape, while the right is not known to admit a (geometric) primitive. No configuration of seven units exposes a nontrivial divisor, because there is none to exhibit. Primality must be established by exhaustive elimination, carried as a conclusion. The absence marks a lack of structure, independent of any gap in knowledge; this makes the witness asymmetry precise.

The lack of nontrivial factors is carried through a construction whose decisive relation is coprimality. Euclid forms a number differing by one from a common multiple of the assigned primes. Any assigned prime that measured both numbers would also measure their unit difference; none can therefore measure the constructed number. Whether that number is prime or is measured by another prime, the assigned multitude is refuted as exhaustive. The result is a reusable refutation schema, not an existential claim over a completed domain.

The modern restatement promotes this refutation schema into a positive claim over the domain of primes. The conversion introduces no immediate contradiction. Here “conservative” has a diagnostic meaning distinct from a proved conservative-extension theorem: the promotion preserves local practice while altering the semantic status of the object introduced. Its apparent harmlessness makes the move easy to absorb and repeat at a larger scale.

By the early 20th century, tensions of this kind could no longer be dismissed as a quirk of exposition [Gödel, 1931] [Heyting, 1931]. A theory may be locally impeccable and still carry a commitment in the way its existential conclusions are understood: a commitment that exceeds what the underlying procedure licenses. The difficulty is that the classical statement is also an epistemic posture, as the initial benign promissory reification at the level of a number has been extended to the entire collection.

This identifies a pattern that recurs wherever local witness is taken to license global closure. A finite, positive, checkable construction may establish a local result cleanly, while the passage to a universal or completed claim is sustained by a structural assumption that the construction itself does not supply. Wherever a finite procedure is promoted into a statement about a total domain, the passage carries implicit weight.

2 Characterization

Reflexica is an interdisciplinary idea at the intersection of formal logic, natural sciences, and metamathematics. It investigates when empirical systems, models, and explanations treat the outputs or limits of local procedures as evidence for a completed domain not independently warranted by those procedures. Its central claim is that a systematic asymmetry may arise between locally witnessed procedures and the global closure conditions those procedures are taken to establish. In the cases considered below, this boundary often becomes visible only indirectly: through reification, self-relation, or obstruction at the point where local procedure is asked to support closure. The asymmetry does not impair mathematics or the sciences as they are ordinarily practiced; it becomes consequential at the periphery, where hard problems, foundational questions, and metamathematical phenomena converge, and the unexamined passage from local witness to global closure begins to carry implicit structural weight.

Reflexica adopts a diagnostic posture alongside existing foundations and calculi, including Martin-Löf Type Theory [Martin-Löf, 1984], Bishop-Style Constructive Mathematics [Bishop, 1967], and constructive presentations of Homotopy Type Theory [Univalent Foundations Program, 2013]. It applies across settings, including classical and empirical ones, to mark the transition from local witness to global closure wherever that transition bears weight.

2.1 The Zeroth World

Impagliazzo's Possible-Worlds Taxonomy [Impagliazzo, 1995] names possible computational worlds by the structural relations among average-case hardness, one-way functions, public-key cryptography, and related complexity assumptions. The Zeroth World lies outside that taxonomy and names the missing or uninhabited position from which Reflexica asks why an unresolved method, once stabilized as a problem, can begin to function as semantic architecture.

The obstruction concerns a structural absence beyond the technical limitations of particular formalisms or their theoreticians: a gap that certain classical principles reify locally, converting it into a reflection principle against which the system recurses on itself, rather than making contact with an independent domain that could ground the closure.

When does a system treat the success of its local operations as empirical warrant for a closure claim about the system, its domain, or an independently existing structure?

The difficulty lies in the conservativeness of the promotion. At a specific threshold, a theory begins to produce what functions as an essence claim after its intended closure has already been reached, or to seek a proposition that restores a transitivity the system has quietly lost. Neither move announces itself as a departure. Each reads as ordinary mathematical practice. It is this readability that allows the passage to go unmarked.

2.2 Methodology

We propose the following core methodological principles:

  • Limitative Abduction: infer not a hidden object, but a boundary condition, from repeated failure of a procedure to furnish the object its own semantics appear to require.
  • Mechanization, subject to exposition: where possible, express the suspected passage as a formal or computational dependency; where formalization remains unavailable, mark the claim as programmatic.
  • Parsimonious Semantics: do not introduce semantic objects beyond what the local procedure, witness, or model licenses, unless the promotion is made explicit; compare predicative restrictions on admissible definitions and domains [Feferman, 1964].

These extend to the following characteristic preliminary instruments:

  • Problem Critique examines the substrate choices and commitments through which a problem or a field is constituted.
  • Semantic Liminality denotes a site where problems from distinct formal domains share propositional logic.
  • Sublimations are irreversible transitions from a formal to an empirical regime.

3 Topics

The cases below are not presented as identical instances. They move from a sensitizing pattern through structured and formal cases, then widen into schematic and conjectural extensions of a proposed passage: local practice encounters an absence, obstruction, or bounded procedure; that local condition is stabilized or reified; and a demand for semantic closure is then placed upon it.

3.1 Mackie’s Error Theory as Sensitizing Pattern

Before turning to more formal cases, Mackie’s Error Theory [Mackie, 1977] serves as a sensitizing pattern outside the article’s mathematical examples. It helps isolate the kind of movement Reflexica is concerned with: a practice encounters an absence, stabilizes that absence through its own internal demands, and at some threshold permits the absence to appear as a semantic object. Mackie’s Queerness Argument therefore orients the reader toward the proposed structure without proving it.

In the Mackiean sense, “queerness” concerns the metaphysical and epistemological strangeness of objective values. It serves here as a way of training attention toward a transition in which the absence of a required semantic term is disciplined by a practice and may then be treated as though it already named an object.

If the semantic object required by realist moral discourse could be produced in the relevant sense, Mackie’s Error Theory would fail; if the theory is correct, no such object is available to be produced. Its correctness therefore cannot take the form of a positive witness for the very object whose absence it asserts. What it marks is an absence whose being witnessed would undermine the thesis that identified it.

We therefore treat Mackie’s Queerness Argument as an indirect and defeasible indication of absence. Its role remains orienting rather than formally instantiating. Where a discourse requires a queer semantic object, failure to produce or locate that object may indicate more than a remediable gap.

The case attunes the reader to a possible movement from an internally stabilized absence to a purported semantic object, without establishing that the later formal cases share its structure. Whether the same passage recurs under more exact conditions remains the question that the following sections investigate.

3.2 Newcomb's Problem

Newcomb's Problem [Nozick, 1969] gives a more structured case. Here the issue is no longer moral objectivity, but the promotion of a locally stipulated predictor into a total evaluator over a class of evaluator-sensitive agents. Commonly treated within decision theory as a question of causal rationality, it is examined here through Problem Critique.

The agent may either “one-box”, receiving $B$, or “two-box”, receiving $A+B$, where $A=1000\,\mathrm{USD}$ and $B\in\{0,10^6\}\,\mathrm{USD}$ has already been fixed by a predictor $P$. Specifically, $B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}$ iff $P$ predicted one-boxing, and $B=0$ iff $P$ predicted two-boxing. Conditional on the value of $B$ being fixed, two-boxing strictly dominates one-boxing by $1000\,\mathrm{USD}$. However, let $C_1$ be the act of one-boxing and $C_2$ the act of two-boxing. If $p=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_1)$ and $q=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_2)$, then $\operatorname{EU}(C_1)=10^6p$ and $\operatorname{EU}(C_2)=1000+10^6q$. Hence one-boxing maximizes evidential expected utility iff $p-q>10^{-3}$.

Causal Decision Theory (CDT) [Lewis, 1981] treats $B$ as fixed and recommends $C_2$, two-boxing. Evidential Decision Theory (EDT) [Jeffrey, 1965] treats the act as evidence about the predictor's prior classification and recommends $C_1$, one-boxing, provided $p-q>10^{-3}$. Both calculations are locally coherent. Neither addresses the prior question: whether the object being optimized over is well-formed.

We focus on the fact that the predictor is internalized as an evaluator over the same domain that contains the agent's decision procedure. The deeper problem concerns the formation of $C$ under the condition that $P$ evaluates it, while $P$ is simultaneously treated as ranging over such evaluator-sensitive choices. The object of evaluation is therefore not independent of the evaluating setup.

The dependency can be displayed schematically:

$$ P:\mathcal{C}(P)\to\{C_1,C_2\}, \quad C\in\mathcal{C}(P). $$

The dependency has an impredicative form: the choice is drawn from a space whose admissible description is already parameterized by the predictor that evaluates it. The agent's choice appears as an object partially constituted by its relation to the predictor, rather than as a first-order act within a fixed environment. Once this is admitted, the predictor assumes a constitutive role beyond that of an ordinary antecedent.

The familiar disagreement between Causal Decision Theory and Evidential Decision Theory is therefore downstream. CDT restores predicativity by imposing a causal type distinction: the box is fixed, the act comes later, and no present intervention reaches backward. EDT instead collapses the predictor and the agent into one correlated evidential structure. Each response selects a different cut through the same impredicative substrate. Neither explains why such a substrate should have been admitted.

Reflexica's Problem Critique rejects this promotion. The first-order mechanism is admissible. What is not licensed is the promotion of a locally stipulated evaluator into a total evaluator over an open-ended class of agents whose decisions are themselves conditioned by that evaluation. A reliable predictor may still be stipulated externally for a particular case. The unlicensed totalization is precisely the passage from local procedure to global closure at issue here.

What remains is a bounded decision problem. One may specify a particular agent, a predictor with stated reliability over a fixed hypothesis class, and an explicit payoff structure; within that model, one may ask which action maximizes a selected decision rule, how calibration changes evidential and causal dependencies, and how robust the recommendation is under changes to the model. The restriction preserves prediction, choice, and decision theory within bounded models. It denies only the unlicensed promotion of a locally specified predictive mechanism into a total evaluator of an open-ended class of evaluator-sensitive agents.

3.3 Diagonalization as Diagnosis

This section treats the classical provability predicate as a formal instance of how a locally witnessed procedure is promoted into a global semantic surrogate, and the resulting obstruction becomes visible only when the surrogate is forced into self-relation.

A prominent interpretive tradition foregrounds diagonalization and self-reference as the source of the Gödelian obstruction [Hofstadter, 1979]. Here the direction is reversed. Diagonalization does not introduce the defect; it exposes a promotion that was already present. The arithmetized provability predicate contains a numerical proxy for proofhood, sustained by an external interpretation of codes as proofs; the Hilbert–Bernays–Löb conditions govern the formal behavior of that predicate. Self-reference exposes a prior semantic promotion: the replacement of proofhood by coded provability, and then of coded provability by a modal or semantic surrogate for proof itself.

Incompleteness instead indicates that the coded predicate never internally contained the semantic order attributed to it.

The legitimate core is local and procedural. Given a finite derivation $\pi$, one can check whether $\pi$ is a valid $T$-proof of a formula $\varphi$. Given a standard Gödel number $n$, one can decode it and verify whether it represents such a derivation.

Thus the proof relation $\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner)$ is not itself suspicious. It is a decidable arithmetical relation over finite codes [Boolos et al., 2007]. The problem begins when this local, checkable relation is promoted into a semantic account of proofhood.

Arithmetized provability is usually defined as:

$$ \operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner) \;:=\; \exists n\,\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner). $$

This formula says that there exists a number satisfying the proof predicate. It does not directly say that a proof-object has been given. The semantic shift occurs when this existential over codes is read as the assertion that $\varphi$ is provable.

Metatheoretically, the existential over proof codes can be decomposed by introducing an actual decoded sequence $\pi$: for a number $n$ to be a proof-code, it must decode into a sequence whose entries are formulas and whose steps are valid applications of the inference rules.

$$ \exists n\,\exists\pi\in\operatorname{Seq}_{\mathrm{fin}}\, \bigl(\operatorname{Decode}(n)=\pi \wedge \operatorname{ValidProofSeq}_T(\pi,\varphi)\bigr). $$

This is a metatheoretic decomposition, not an additional existential formulated inside first-order arithmetic. Within the arithmetized setting, $\pi$ is itself represented numerically; the displayed sequence belongs to the external interpretation of the code.

The arithmetized provability predicate does not contain proofhood. It contains a numerical proxy for proofhood, plus an external convention under which certain numbers are read as proofs.

The Hilbert–Bernays–Löb Derivability Conditions are formally justified [Boolos, 1993]. They express the fact that coded provability is closed under certain proof-code transformations. But this is not the same as giving a semantics of proof-inhabitation.

For example, the condition

$$ T\vdash \operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner) \rightarrow \operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}} \bigl(\ulcorner\operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner)\urcorner\bigr) $$

need not mean that a proof reflects on itself. It means that from a code satisfying the proof predicate one can construct another code satisfying the proof predicate for an existential claim about the first code.

HBL therefore gives closure of coded provability under coded proof-transformations. It does not, by itself, give an inhabited semantics of proofhood.

In a nonstandard model $M\models T$, the model may satisfy [Kaye, 1991]:

$$ M\models \exists n\,\operatorname{Proof}_T(n,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner). $$

But the witness may be nonstandard. Externally, it need not correspond to a genuine finite proof. Internally, the existential is inhabited; externally, the alleged proof may be non-extractable.

More precisely, there may be a nonstandard element $a\in M$ such that $M\models\operatorname{Proof}_T(a,\ulcorner\varphi\urcorner)$, while externally $a$ does not code any standard finite derivation.

The Gödel Sentence has the form:

$$ \mathsf{G}\iff \neg\operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner \mathsf{G} \urcorner). $$

On the usual reading, the pathology lies in self-reference. On the Reflexica reading, diagonalization is not the pathology. It is the diagnostic device that reveals the prior promotion of coded provability into semantic proofhood.

The Gödel Sentence forces the provability predicate to occupy the position of proofhood itself. The resulting obstruction is not accidental. It marks the failure of the surrogate to contain the semantic core it was made to represent.

Diagonalization is powerful here because its disclosure does not depend on the complete legitimacy of the semantic predicate it targets. Its force persists across two opposed outcomes.

If the provability predicate is treated as merely formal or promissory, the diagonal exposes the gap between coded provability and genuine proofhood. The sentence

$$ \mathsf{G}\iff \neg\operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner \mathsf{G} \urcorner) $$

then functions diagnostically: the surrogate fails to capture the sentence through the semantic depth it was taken to represent. This branch identifies a representational gap rather than establishing an arithmetical truth:

$$ \text{semantic inadequacy} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{diagnosis of the surrogate}. $$

If, on the other hand, the classical semantic reading is granted—standard arithmetic, standard proof codes, and ordinary model-theoretic truth—then the same diagonal sentence is true in the usual Gödelian sense. Under the usual assumptions on $T$ [Boolos, 1993], $\mathsf{G}$ is not provable in $T$; because this is what $\mathsf{G}$ asserts under the standard classical interpretation, $\mathsf{G}$ is true:

$$ \mathsf{G} \; \text{expresses an unprovable arithmetical truth}. $$

The two outcomes must remain distinct. Semantic adequacy yields the classical incompleteness result; semantic inadequacy yields a metatheoretic diagnosis of the representational gap.

The diagonal discloses a boundary whether the semantic predicate proves adequate or fails in the role assigned to it.

The proposed invariant is therefore disclosure rather than truth. If the surrogate is semantically adequate, the diagonal exposes its incompleteness. If the surrogate fails to represent proofhood adequately, the diagonal exposes that failure. It identifies the boundary either as incompleteness within an adequate interpretation or as inadequacy in the interpretation itself.

Constructive Logic is attractive here because it refuses to let existence float free of witnesshood. An existential claim $\exists x:A\,P(x)$ must be inhabited by data, typically a pair consisting of a witness and evidence that the witness satisfies the predicate.

$$ (a,p):\sum_{x:A}P(x) $$

This does not eliminate limitative phenomena: Constructive Logic requires the semantic cost of existence claims to be paid in witnesses (or assumptions) rather than deferred into an ambient ontology of completed domains.

3.4 Self-Reference as a Schema

After diagonalization has been treated as the formal diagnostic case, self-reference can be considered more broadly as a schema. The question is whether certain forms of self-reference reproduce the same configuration: a local procedure is asked to survey, name, or close over the totality to which it itself belongs.

This transfer from the strict diagonal case to less formal settings is programmatic. A structural resonance across algebra, computation, linguistic competence, and proof theory is not yet a proof that these domains instantiate one phenomenon. To assert such an identity without a witness would perform the totalizing move Reflexica is intended to diagnose.

Reflexica therefore does not claim that self-reference always causes obstruction or that every self-survey is a diagonal argument. It asks whether self-description, self-embedding, or self-similarity may reproduce a related demand for closure in domains that share no objects, methods, or historical contact at the relevant points of development [Lawvere, 1969].

Consider, as a schematic case, a Turing machine [Turing, 1937]

$$ \mathcal{M}=(Q,\Sigma,\Gamma,\delta,q_0,q_{\mathrm{acc}},q_{\mathrm{rej}}), $$

with language

$$ L(\mathcal{M})=\{w\in\Sigma^*: \mathcal{M}\text{ accepts }w\}. $$

The language is not an internal component of the transition table. In a Tarskian register, the distinction is between the machine's formal specification and a semantic extension assigned at the metalevel [Gruber, 2016].

A particular computation may witness that a string belongs to $L(\mathcal{M})$. The language itself, however, is not another term available to the computation. It is the extension obtained when the machine's activity is taken as a whole. A self-description remains a presentation of the rule; a stream of accepted strings remains a sequence of witnesses. Neither returns the extension as an object internal to the activity that constitutes it.

A related question posed to a human provides a second schematic transfer.

How many words do you know?

The question is well formed, but an exact answer need not be available to the subject. Producing one would require an exhaustive, fixed inventory of an evolving and context-sensitive linguistic competence; the survey would be conducted by the very system whose contents were being inventoried. What returns is often an estimate rather than a retrieved census.

A language model asked the same question may also return an estimate. Such an estimate should be understood as a locally generated response rather than a retrieved census. Neither the human nor the language-model case establishes an identity with Gödelian diagonalization; each serves as a test of whether self-reference outside the strict diagonal case can reproduce a structurally related limit of self-survey.

The possible resonance with diagonalization appears only when that activity is required to yield its own closure. The machine–human–language-model comparison does not prove a common origin; it marks a question about recurrence: whether the demand presupposes a separation between the local procedure and the semantic extension constituted by taking that procedure as a whole.

3.5 Almost Saturated Fields

We consider the passage from proposition to runtime. Each transition selects one inhabitation of the space above it and discards what the layer below need not distinguish to proceed. The compression is constitutive, not incidental.

$$ P \;\xrightarrow{\;\lambda\;}\; \lambda x.\,t \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{compile}\;}\; s \;\xrightarrow{\;\text{eval}\;}\; r $$

Under the equivalences imposed at each stage, these arrows may be many-to-one: different proofs may determine extensionally equivalent terms, different terms may compile to behaviorally equivalent states, and different states may evaluate to the same result. A lossless reversal would require preserving distinctions that the lower layer has already discarded. The obstacle is therefore not merely computational cost, but quotienting: the system proceeds by identifying cases that the previous layer could still distinguish.

Blum-Style Speedup results show that, for suitable complexity measures, there exist computable predicates for which no single program is optimal: any proposed implementation can be improved upon in the relevant formal sense [Blum, 1967]. A finite system reflecting on its own procedures can treat this as a model for improvement: self-reflection produces a stronger description, and the stronger description may yield a faster inhabitation of the same type. But the rate at which reflection produces distinguishable gain is itself subject to the same compression. Each reflective step purchases less potential than the last.

Let $(G_n)$ denote the improvement distinguishable by the system after $n$ rounds of reflection, relative to its current equivalence relation on procedures. The claim needed here is only diminishing distinguishable gain,

$$ \Delta_n=G_{n+1}-G_n,\quad \Delta_n\ge 0,\quad \Delta_n\to 0. $$

A logarithmic envelope,

$$ G_n=\alpha\log(n+1)+\beta,\qquad \alpha>0, $$

may be introduced only as a proposed model of diminishing marginal gain, not as a theorem following from speedup alone.

This behavior is called “almost saturation” here. The term does not denote convergence to a mathematical fixed point. Let $\sim_S$ denote the system's internal indistinguishability relation at its current evaluative level. Operational saturation occurs at stage $n$ when

$$ G_{n+1}\sim_S G_n. $$

If this relation is represented by a fixed positive resolution threshold $\varepsilon>0$, a sufficient condition is

$$ \Delta_n<\varepsilon. $$

For the logarithmic model,

$$ \Delta_n =\alpha\log\!\left(\frac{n+2}{n+1}\right) \longrightarrow 0. $$

The sequence $G_n$ remains unbounded, while its increments eventually fall below every fixed positive resolution threshold. The conjecture is that a reflective system can therefore become saturated relative to its own evaluative equivalence without exhausting the space of possible improvements.

On the proposed interpretation, locating the precise moment of promotion would require a witness outside the closure. The conjecture is that the question of where the metric became the ceiling cannot be answered from within the system asking it: a reflective system seeking the boundary of its own operational saturation remains bounded by the evaluative equivalence it seeks to locate.

4 The Program

The cases above share no substrate, method, or historical contact at the relevant points. What they share, if the proposal is correct, is a configuration: local production, self-relation or self-survey, demand for closure, and an ensuing reification or obstruction. Reflexica therefore proposes, rather than establishes, a structural position: recurrent self-relation, whether through self-survey or self-similar expansion, may coincide with a formation limit of related form. If such an obstruction is substrate-independent, then tools first developed in formal settings may have disciplined analogues outside those settings. The transfer, however, is itself a Reflexica-sensitive move: it must be marked, not assumed.

Classical totalities and completed domains are undeniably productive, and their local utility is not in question, as the expansion to the empirical realm is the program's main purpose. The program examines neuronal computation, evolutionary closure, and self-modeling in artificial systems, asking how they relate to classical undecidability.

The epistemically proper form is consequently not:

These are all reflexica.

but:

Under what conditions would these count as the same phenomenon?

To assert their identity in advance would transform a series of bounded observations into an unwitnessed totality claim—the very passage Reflexica seeks to mark. The resonance therefore identifies a frontier at which formal logic, metamathematics, and the sciences of complex self-modeling systems converge on a question that none of them, separately, has the resources to settle under consistency.

Our question then becomes sharper:

Is reflexica inherited?

It asks whether a structural configuration—local production, self-relation or self-survey, a demand for closure, and an ensuing unwitnessed reification or obstruction—is preserved across a change of substrate. If it is, then Reflexica may name a substrate-independent limitative structure. If it is not, then the apparent resonance belongs to our description of the cases rather than to the cases themselves. Whether these are again an instance of Reflexica is the main object of study.

These questions are left open as the correct epistemic form for claims at the boundary of closure.
