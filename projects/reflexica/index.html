Abstract

Across logic-adjacent contemporary fields, a locally witnessed procedure is often quietly promoted into a claim about a completed domain. This operation may recur in mathematics, semantics, computation, and empirical inquiry. The problematic promotion introduces no immediate contradiction, at first.

Reflexica is a program for making this passage visible, examining the conditions under which it occurs, and asking what forms of obstruction appear when a system becomes related to what it cannot witness.

What is Reflexica?

1 Introduction

Reflexica begins from an intuitionistic standpoint [Brouwer, 1913].

Read procedurally, Book IX, Proposition 20 of Euclid's Elements [Euclid, 1956] does not begin from a completed set of primes.

It supplies an extension rule:

“Therefore $G$ is not the same with any one of the numbers $A, B,$ and $C$. And by hypothesis it is prime. Therefore the prime numbers $A, B, C,$ and $G$ have been found which are more than the assigned multitude of $A, B,$ and $C$.”

From any assigned finite multitude of primes, one constructs a number coprime to every member of that multitude. The proposition is an iterable and open-ended procedure before the contemporary presentation converts it into a claim about a completed domain. The difference concerns metamathematical commitment rather than attitude.

The procedural reading is answerable to explicit construction: it commits only to what that construction reaches, and no further. The existential reading permits the result to be expressed over a completed domain, the primes as a total collection, rather than as an indefinitely iterable extension operation. One reading foregrounds the construction by which a further prime divisor is obtained; the other licenses quantification over the resulting domain. Both readings are valid, though they carry different proof-theoretic and semantic commitments.

Classically, this often appears cost-free: the existential formulation preserves the theorem while suppressing the difference between a proposition proved in a completed domain and a witness extracted from a construction. The contrast is between a classically accepted existential $\exists x:A\,P(x)$ and its constructive interpretation as data: an object $a:A$ together with evidence for $P(a)$. Under propositions-as-types, this is represented by an inhabitant of $\sum_{x:A}P(x)$ [Howard, 1980]. The relevant distinction here is between proof-theoretic signatures [Gentzen, 1935]. To establish that a number is composite, one produces a factor greater than one: a finite, extractable, checkable object. The witness supplies the computational content of the proof; in the case of compositeness, a factor pair $(a,b)$ is the finite object whose verification establishes the claim [Kleene, 1945]. Numbers alone, in a constructive sense, do not carry such a witness; it must be decided for them.

The asymmetry is visible geometrically in the simple case below:

$$ \begin{array}{ll} 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+1+1\\ 1+1+1 & \qquad 1+0+0\\[2ex] \exists \, \mathrm{d}_{i} >1 & \qquad ? \end{array} $$

We write schematically. Compositeness has the form

$$ \operatorname{Comp}(n) \;\iff\; \exists a,b\,(1<a<n \wedge 1<b<n \wedge n=ab), $$

so a proof may present a finite factor witness $(a,b)$. Primality is naturally expressed by bounded exclusion:

$$ \operatorname{Prime}(n) \;\iff\; n>1 \wedge \forall d\,(1<d<n \rightarrow d\nmid n). $$

The left arrangement witnesses factorization directly as the factor is read off the shape, while the right is not known to admit a (geometric) primitive. No configuration of seven units exposes a nontrivial divisor, because there is none to exhibit. Primality must be established by exhaustive elimination, carried as a conclusion rather than read off as a positive configuration. The absence marks a lack of factor structure, independent of any gap in knowledge.

The lack of nontrivial factors is carried through a construction whose decisive relation is coprimality. Euclid forms a number differing by one from a common multiple of the assigned primes. Any assigned prime that measured both numbers would also measure their unit difference; none can therefore measure the constructed number. Whether that number is prime or is measured by another prime, the assigned multitude is refuted as exhaustive. The result is a reusable refutation schema, not an existential claim over a completed domain.

The modern restatement promotes this refutation schema into a positive claim over the domain of primes. The conversion introduces no immediate contradiction. Here “conservative” has a diagnostic meaning distinct from a proved conservative-extension theorem: the promotion preserves local practice while altering the semantic status of the object introduced. Its apparent harmlessness makes the move easy to absorb and repeat at a larger scale.

By the early 20th century, tensions of this kind could no longer be dismissed as a quirk of exposition [Gödel, 1931] [Heyting, 1931]. A theory may be locally impeccable and still carry a commitment in the way its existential conclusions are understood: a commitment that exceeds what the underlying procedure licenses. The difficulty is that the classical statement is also an epistemic posture, as the initial benign promissory reification at the level of a number has been extended to the entire collection.

This identifies a pattern that recurs wherever local witness is taken to license global closure. A finite, positive, checkable construction may establish a local result cleanly, while the passage to a universal or completed claim is sustained by a structural assumption that the construction itself does not supply. Wherever a finite procedure is promoted into a statement about a total domain, the passage carries implicit weight.

2 Characterization

Reflexica is an interdisciplinary idea at the intersection of formal logic, natural sciences, and metamathematics. It investigates when empirical systems, models, and explanations treat the outputs or limits of local procedures as evidence for a completed domain not independently warranted by those procedures. Its central claim is that a systematic asymmetry may arise between locally witnessed procedures and the global closure conditions those procedures are taken to establish. In the cases considered below, this boundary often becomes visible only indirectly: through reification, self-relation, or obstruction at the point where local procedure is asked to support closure.

The above does not impair mathematics or the sciences as they are ordinarily practiced; it becomes consequential at the periphery, where hard problems, foundational questions, and metamathematical phenomena converge, and the unexamined passage from local witness to global closure begins to carry implicit structural weight.

One does not need to replace existing foundations and calculi, including Martin-Löf Type Theory [Martin-Löf, 1984], Bishop-Style Constructive Mathematics [Bishop, 1967], and constructive presentations of Homotopy Type Theory [Univalent Foundations Program, 2013].

2.1 The Zeroth World

The name “Reflexica” is an antiphrasis borrowing from Impagliazzo's Possible-Worlds Taxonomy in average-case complexity [Impagliazzo, 1995], projecting a missing “uninhabited world.”

We use this image to ask why a lack of method, denoted as problems, is converted into semantic architecture at all. Complexity theory enters here as both object and symptom: a field whose dominant formulations may stall when coupled to a contradiction-free classical totality, and thence, no contradiction follows, because the promotion remains conservative: an existential claim can be read back into its procedural counterpart.

The obstruction concerns a structural absence beyond the technical limitations of particular formalisms or their theoreticians: a gap that certain classical principles reify locally, converting it into a reflection principle against which the system recurses on itself, rather than making contact with an independent domain that could ground the closure.

When does a system treat the success of its local operations as empirical warrant for a closure claim about the system, its domain, or an independently existing structure?

The difficulty lies in the conservativeness of the promotion. At a specific threshold, a theory begins to produce what functions as an essence claim after its intended closure has already been reached, or to seek a proposition that restores a transitivity the system has quietly lost. Neither move announces itself as a departure. Each reads as ordinary mathematical practice. It is this readability that allows the passage to go unmarked.

2.2 Methodology

We propose the following core methodological principles:

  • Limitative Abduction: infer not a hidden object, but a boundary condition, from repeated failure of a procedure to furnish the object its own semantics appear to require.
  • Mechanization, subject to exposition: where possible, express the suspected passage as a formal or computational dependency; where formalization remains unavailable, mark the claim as programmatic.
  • Parsimonious Semantics: do not introduce semantic objects beyond what the local procedure, witness, or model licenses, unless the promotion is made explicit; compare predicative restrictions on admissible definitions and domains [Feferman, 1964].

These extend to the following characteristic preliminary higher-order instruments:

  • Problem Critique examines the substrate choices and commitments through which a problem or a field is constituted. A problem cannot be admitted until the partials that constitute it have been made explicit and witnessed.
  • Semantic Liminality denotes a site where problems from distinct formal domains share propositional logic.
  • Sublimations are irreversible transitions from a formal to an empirical regime.

3 Topics

The cases below are not presented as identical instances. They move from a sensitizing pattern through structured and formal cases, then widen into schematic and conjectural extensions of a proposed passage: local practice encounters an absence, obstruction, or bounded procedure; that local condition is stabilized or reified; and a demand for semantic closure is then placed upon it.

3.1 Mackie’s Error Theory as Sensitizing Pattern

Mackie’s Error Theory [Mackie, 1977] serves here as a sensitizing pattern rather than a formal case. The movement it illustrates is this: a practice encounters an absence, stabilizes that absence through its own internal demands, and at some threshold permits the absence to appear as a semantic object. The Queerness Argument names the strangeness of the moral properties that realist discourse appears to require, properties that are neither straightforwardly natural nor straightforwardly constructed.

The Reflexica point is precise. If the error-theoretic diagnosis is correct, it cannot be confirmed by producing the objective moral property whose existence it denies. Its correctness therefore cannot take the form of a positive witness for the very object whose absence it asserts. What the argument marks is a shadow: something whose being exhibited would undermine the thesis that identified it. The absence is not merely a gap in the inventory but becomes theoretically active inside the practice it diagnoses.

Whether the same passage recurs under more exact conditions is what the following sections investigate. That it appears in other phenomenological discussions suggests the pattern is not local to metaethics, though that suggestion remains programmatic here.

3.2 Newcomb's Problem

We move to a more structured case: Newcomb's Problem [Nozick, 1969].

Here the issue is no longer objectivity, but the promotion of a locally stipulated predictor into a total evaluator over a class of evaluator-sensitive agents. Commonly treated within decision theory as a question of causal rationality, it is examined here through Problem Critique.

The agent may either “one-box”, receiving $B$, or “two-box”, receiving $A+B$, where $A=1000\,\mathrm{USD}$ and $B\in\{0,10^6\}\,\mathrm{USD}$ has already been fixed by a predictor $P$. Specifically, $B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}$ iff $P$ predicted one-boxing, and $B=0$ iff $P$ predicted two-boxing. Conditional on the value of $B$ being fixed, two-boxing strictly dominates one-boxing by $1000\,\mathrm{USD}$. However, let $C_1$ be the act of one-boxing and $C_2$ the act of two-boxing. If $p=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_1)$ and $q=\Pr(B=10^6\,\mathrm{USD}\mid C_2)$, then $\operatorname{EU}(C_1)=10^6p$ and $\operatorname{EU}(C_2)=1000+10^6q$. Hence one-boxing maximizes evidential expected utility iff $p-q>10^{-3}$.

Causal Decision Theory (CDT) [Lewis, 1981] treats $B$ as fixed and recommends $C_2$, two-boxing. Evidential Decision Theory (EDT) [Jeffrey, 1965] treats the act as evidence about the predictor's prior classification and recommends $C_1$, one-boxing, provided $p-q>10^{-3}$. Both calculations are locally coherent. Neither addresses the prior question: whether the object being optimized over is well-formed.

The reflexive structure of Newcomb's Problem is not merely that the predictor and the agent may simulate one another. More precisely, the problem hides a diagonal construction. The predictor is applied to a description of the very decision procedure whose output may be defined in terms of that prediction.

Let $O(x)$ say that the decision procedure coded by $x$ one-boxes, and let $P(x)$ say that the predictor predicts that the procedure coded by $x$ one-boxes. A predictor-sensitive decision schema may then be represented as

$$ O(x)\leftrightarrow F(P(x)), $$

where $F$ specifies how the agent responds to the predictor's classification. By the diagonal construction, there is a code $d$ such that

$$ O(d)\leftrightarrow F(P(d)). $$

Thus the relevant object is not an independently formed act subsequently classified by $P$. It is a fixed point obtained by substituting the agent's own description into a predictor-sensitive schema. Newcomb's Problem stipulates that the large box contains $10^6\,\mathrm{USD}$ iff the predictor predicted one-boxing; it therefore evaluates $P(d)$, the very predicate already occurring inside the formation of $O(d)$.

This is why the usual causal/evidential disagreement is downstream of the FOL semantics. CDT treats the box content as fixed; EDT treats the act as evidence about the prior prediction. But both calculations presuppose that the self-substituted object $d$ has already been admitted as a well-formed target of evaluation. The problem is therefore a hidden Diagonal Lemma: a locally stipulated predictor is promoted into a total evaluator over a domain closed under procedures that can refer to that very evaluation.

Reflexica's Problem Critique rejects this promotion. The first-order mechanism is admissible; what is not licensed is the promotion of a locally stipulated evaluator into a total evaluator over an open-ended class of agents whose decisions are themselves conditioned by that evaluation. A reliable predictor may still be stipulated externally for a particular case. The unlicensed totalization is precisely the passage from local procedure to global closure at issue here.

What remains is a bounded decision problem. One may specify a particular agent, a predictor with stated reliability over a fixed hypothesis class, and an explicit payoff structure; within that model, one may ask which action maximizes a selected decision rule, how calibration changes evidential and causal dependencies, and how robust the recommendation is under changes to the model. The restriction preserves prediction, choice, and decision theory within bounded models. It denies only the unlicensed promotion of a locally specified predictive mechanism into a total evaluator of an open-ended class of evaluator-sensitive agents.

3.3 Diagonalization and Diagnosis

This section treats Gödelian Diagonalization not as a local trick of self-reference, but as a diagnostic obstruction at the level of proof itself [Gödel, 1931] [Boolos et al., 2007].

Consider two opposing pictures of provability.

On an antirealist or broadly constructive picture, arithmetic truth does not outrun proof, warrant, construction, or some admissible form of verification. A mathematical proposition is not treated as determinately true independently of the means by which it can be established.

On a realist (classic) picture, arithmetic truth is fixed independently of proof or warrant. The standard natural numbers form a determinate domain, and arithmetic sentences have determinate truth-values whether or not a given proof-system can establish them.

The difficulty is that we do not get to stand outside both pictures and inspect, from a neutral position, which one is metaphysically correct. Any argument for one picture over the other must itself proceed by some method. Let that method be denoted by $M$.

But $M$ cannot be an inert object. If it is to settle the question, $M$ must be a proof-method, or at least a warrant-conferring method. It must tell us whether arithmetic is to be understood realistically or antirealistically.

That is the trap. To decide between realism and antirealism, we need a proof-method whose authority is not already being interpreted realistically or antirealistically. But no such neutral $M$ is available. If $M$ is understood antirealistically, it cannot establish semantic excess over proof, since such excess is precisely what the antirealist refuses. If $M$ is understood realistically, then it already assumes the semantic authority it was supposed to justify.

Gödel enters exactly here. By the Diagonal Lemma, one can construct a sentence $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ such that

$$ \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}} \leftrightarrow \neg \operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}\urcorner). $$

Informally, $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ says that $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is not provable in $\mathrm{T}$.

This construction is stable across the dispute. Both the realist and the antirealist can recognize the formal construction. Both can accept that, under the relevant consistency assumptions, $\mathrm{T}$ does not prove $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$. The disagreement concerns what follows from this.

In the antirealist picture, Gödel's Theorem remains a genuine formal result. It shows that a sufficiently strong effective proof-system cannot, on pain of inconsistency, prove its Gödel Sentence:

$$ \mathrm{T} \nvdash \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}. $$

But the antirealist does not thereby have to accept the further claim

$$ \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}} \text{ is true but unprovable}. $$

That further claim requires a notion of arithmetic truth that outruns proof or warrant. Yet such a notion is precisely what the antirealist rejects or refuses to treat as primitive. For the antirealist, then, Gödel is not false. It is deflated. It becomes a theorem about the limits of a given formal system, not a revelation of an independently obtaining arithmetical fact.

In this sense $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is vacuous with respect to realism. It cannot prove realism from inside antirealism, because the semantic surplus needed for the realist conclusion has already been disallowed. The theorem still marks a boundary, but the boundary is internal to the formal apparatus. It does not by itself license a metaphysics of completed arithmetic truth.

In the realist picture, the same construction has greater force. If there is a determinate standard model of arithmetic, and if $\mathrm{T}$ is sound with respect to that model, then $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is true because what it says is the case. It says that it is not $\mathrm{T}$-provable, and, under the relevant assumptions, it is not $\mathrm{T}$-provable.

So the same formal construction now yields

$$ \text{truth} \neq \mathrm{T}\text{-provability}. $$

Here $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is not merely a boundary theorem about $\mathrm{T}$. It becomes a method for showing that arithmetic truth exceeds any given sufficiently strong effective formal system. $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is then read as an arithmetical truth not captured by the proof-system whose provability predicate it mentions.

Thus $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ is invariant across the dispute, but its semantics are not. The invariant construction is

$$ \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}} \leftrightarrow \neg \operatorname{Prov}_{\mathrm{T}}(\ulcorner \mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}\urcorner). $$

The two readings are:

$$ \text{consistency boundary of }\mathrm{T}. \; \text{unprovable truth beyond }\mathrm{T}. $$

The formal diagonal does not decide between these readings. It can be accepted in both regimes. What changes is the semantic authority granted to the sentence after the formal result has been obtained.

This is the relevant obstruction. We want to know whether arithmetic belongs to the antirealist regime or to the realist regime. But to prove either answer, we require a method $M$. Once $M$ is introduced, the question repeats:

$$ \text{What is the status of }M? $$

If $M$ is treated antirealistically, then it cannot prove the existence of arithmetic truth beyond proof, since such truth is not admitted as an independent target. If $M$ is treated realistically, then it already presupposes the semantic excess it was meant to establish.

The needed method is therefore exactly what cannot be neutrally supplied.

The structure may be displayed as follows:

$$ \begin{array}{c} \text{Realism vs Antirealism} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{Need a proof-method whose authority is neutral} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{But proof-methods are themselves interpreted inside the dispute} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{2nd Incompleteness Theorem applies in both regimes} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{In antirealism: formal boundary of }\mathrm{T} \\ \text{In realism: semantic excess of arithmetic truth over }\mathrm{T} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{No witness of which world we inhabit.} \\ \Downarrow \\ \text{Reflexica.} \end{array} $$

If $\mathrm{T}$ is read modestly, as a formal calculus, $\mathsf{G}_{\mathrm{T}}$ merely says: this sentence is not derivable in $\mathrm{T}$. Under the usual consistency assumptions, that is a true boundary statement about $\mathrm{T}$. No scandal follows. $\mathrm{T}$ remains a well-defined syntactic object.

If $\mathrm{T}$ is read ambitiously, as capturing proofhood, the same sentence becomes decisive. It is now a truth whose non-derivability separates proof from $\mathrm{T}$-derivation. Thus the diagonal is stronger than an objection to $\mathrm{T}$: it works whether $\mathrm{T}$ is treated as empty syntax or as ideal proof. In the first case it marks a limit; in the second it refutes the promotion.

The point is that the demand for a proof of the correct theory of proof is itself caught in the dispute it seeks to resolve. Gödel's Theorem is stable under both realism and antirealism, but it cannot neutrally choose between them, because the choice requires the very semantic authority under dispute.

The diagonal construction therefore diagnoses a limit not only of $\mathrm{T}$, but of the attempt to adjudicate the status of proof from a position outside proof. Once a method is asked to certify the correct interpretation of method itself, it is no longer neutral.

3.4 Self-Reference as a Schema

After diagonalization has been treated as the formal diagnostic case, self-reference can be considered more broadly as a schema. The question is whether certain forms of self-reference reproduce the same configuration: a local procedure is asked to survey, name, or close over the totality to which it itself belongs.

This transfer from the strict diagonal case to less formal settings is programmatic. A structural resonance across algebra, computation, linguistic competence, and proof theory is not yet a proof that these domains instantiate one phenomenon. To assert such an identity without a witness would perform the totalizing move Reflexica is intended to diagnose.

Reflexica therefore does not claim that self-reference always causes obstruction or that every self-survey is a diagonal argument. It asks whether self-description, self-embedding, or self-similarity may reproduce a related demand for closure in domains that share no objects, methods, or historical contact at the relevant points of development [Lawvere, 1969].

Consider, as a schematic case, a Turing machine [Turing, 1937]

$$ \mathcal{M}=(Q,\Sigma,\Gamma,\delta,q_0,q_{\mathrm{acc}},q_{\mathrm{rej}}), $$

with language

$$ L(\mathcal{M})=\{w\in\Sigma^*: \mathcal{M}\text{ accepts }w\}. $$

The language is not an internal component of the transition table. In a Tarskian register, the distinction is between the machine's formal specification and a semantic extension assigned at the metalevel [Gruber, 2016].

A particular computation may witness that a string belongs to $L(\mathcal{M})$. The language itself, however, is not another term available to the computation. It is the extension obtained when the machine's activity is taken as a whole. A self-description remains a presentation of the rule; a stream of accepted strings remains a sequence of witnesses. Neither returns the extension as an object internal to the activity that constitutes it.

A related question posed to a human provides a second schematic transfer.

How many words do you know?

The question is well formed, but an exact answer need not be available to the subject. Producing one would require an exhaustive, fixed inventory of an evolving and context-sensitive linguistic competence; the survey would be conducted by the very system whose contents were being inventoried. What returns is often an estimate rather than a retrieved census.

A language model asked the same question may also return an estimate. Such an estimate should be understood as a locally generated response rather than a retrieved census. Neither the human nor the language-model case establishes an identity with Gödelian Diagonalization; each serves as a test of whether self-reference outside the strict diagonal case can reproduce a structurally related limit of self-survey.

The possible resonance with diagonalization appears only when that activity is required to yield its own closure. The machine–human–language-model comparison does not prove a common origin; it marks a question about recurrence: whether the demand presupposes a separation between the local procedure and the semantic extension constituted by taking that procedure as a whole.

3.5 Substrate

The cases above share no substrate, method, or historical contact at the relevant points. What they may share is a configuration, and that possibility, rather than an established identity, is what Reflexica proposes to examine. Recurrent self-relation, whether through self-survey or self-similar expansion, may coincide with a formation limit of related form.

The transfer, however, is itself sensitive, and the point requires precision. What connects these cases is not a shared substrate or common historical ancestry. It is a relation: in each, the absence of a witness is not merely an epistemic shortfall but becomes operationally constitutive: the system is put into motion by what it cannot supply.

Euclid's list cannot witness its own totality; the construction opens onto what the list excludes. The Newcomb agent cannot occupy the external position from which its action is a neutral fact; the predictor's model internalizes that action, and the unwitnessed future governs the formation of the decision. Mackie's error-theoretic diagnosis cannot be established by producing the moral property whose absence it identifies; the non-exhibitability is not an obstacle to the argument but its operative core. Gödel's system cannot witness its own truth predicate from within the relevant register; the diagonal sentence places the system in relation to a gap it cannot close from either side. The halting machine asked to decide its own behavior does not merely fail; it becomes entangled with the cases its own operation has generated.

4 The Program

Classical totalities and completed domains are undeniably productive; Reflexica asks what is added when local procedures are read as completed closures, and what forms of obstruction appear when a system becomes related to what it cannot witness.

The program therefore extends the diagnostic beyond formal mathematics without treating the extension as automatic. Its central task is to examine neuronal computation, evolutionary closure, and self-modeling in artificial systems, asking whether these empirical cases exhibit disciplined analogues of classical undecidability, witness failure, and closure obstruction.

The epistemically proper form is consequently not:

These are all reflexica.

but:

Under what conditions would these count as the same phenomenon?

To assert their identity in advance would transform a series of bounded observations into an unwitnessed totality claim, which is the very passage Reflexica seeks to mark. The resonance therefore identifies a frontier at which formal logic, metamathematics, and the sciences of complex self-modeling systems converge on a question that none of them, separately, has the resources to settle under consistency.

Hence, our question then becomes:

Is reflexica inherited?

It asks whether a structural configuration consisting of local production, self-relation or self-survey, a demand for closure, and an ensuing unwitnessed reification or obstruction is preserved across a change of substrate. If it is, then Reflexica may name a substrate-independent limitative structure. If it is not, then the apparent resonance belongs to our description of the cases rather than to the cases themselves. Whether these are again instances of Reflexica is the main object of study.

These questions are left open as the correct epistemic form for claims at the boundary of closure.
